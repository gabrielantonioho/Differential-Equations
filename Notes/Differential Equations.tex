\subsection*{Problems 1, 2, 3, 4}

\DirectionField{problem1.png}

\textbf{Problem 1}: Above shows the direction field for $y' = 3 - y$. It can be seen that for $y > 3$ the slopes are negative so solutions decrease and 
for $y < 3$ the slopes are positive so solutions increase. 
The equilibrium position appears to be at $y(t) = 3$, to which all other solutions converge.

\DirectionField{problem2.png}

\textbf{Problem 2}: Above shows the direction field for $y' = 2y - 4$. For $y > 2$ the slopes are positive so solutions increase 
and for $y < 2$ the slopes are negative so solutions decrease. The equilibrium solution appears to be $y = 2$ and all other solutions diverge away from this solution. 

\DirectionField{problem3.png}

\textbf{Problem 3}: Above shows the direction field for $y' = -1 -4y$. For $y > -\frac{1}{4}$ the slopes are positive so solutions increase. For $ y > -\frac{1}{4} $ the slopes are negative so solutions decrease. The equilibrium solution appears to be $ y = -\frac{1}{4} $ and all other solutions converge to this solution.

\DirectionField{problem4.png}

\textbf{Problem 4}: Above shows the direction field for $y' = 4y - 2$. For $y > 0.5$ the slopes are positive so solutions are increasing. For $ y < 0.5 $ the slopes are negative so the solutions are decreasing. It appears that the equilibrium solution is at $ y = \frac{1}{2} $ and all other solutions diverge away.

\subsection*{Problems 5 and 6}

\textbf{Problem 5:} A differential equation where all solutions approach $y = \frac{2}{3}$ is given by $\frac{dy}{dx} = 2 - 3y$. 

\noindent\textbf{Problem 6:} A differential equations where all other solutions diverge from $y = 3$ is $\frac{dy}{dx} = y - 3$

\subsection*{Problems 7, 8, 9, 10}

\textbf{Problem 7:} 

\DirectionField{problem7.png}

For $y > 4$, the slopes are negative and so solutions decrease. 
For $0 < y < 4$, slopes are positive so solutions increase. 
For $y < 0$ slopes are negative so solutions decrease.
Equilibrium solutions appear to be at $y = 4$ to which solutions converge to and  $y = 0$ at which solutions diverge from.

\textbf{Problem 8:} 

\DirectionField{problem8.png}

For $y > 4$, the slopes are positive and so solutions increase. 
For $0 < y < 4$, slopes are negative so solutions decrease. 
For $y < 0$ slopes are positive so solutions decrease.
Equilibrium solutions appear to be at $y = 0$ to which solutions converge to and  $y = 4$ at which solutions diverge from.

\textbf{Problem 9:} 

\DirectionField{problem9.png}

For $y > 0$, the slopes are positive and so solutions increase. For $y < 0$, the slopes are positive and so solutions increase. There appears to be an equilibrium position at $y = 0$ where solutions greater than 0 diverge from and solutions less than 0 converge to.

\textbf{Problem 10:} 

\DirectionField{problem10.png}

For $y > 1$, the slopes are positive and so solutions are increasing
For $0 < y < 1$, the slopes are positive so solutions are increasing
For $y < 0$, the slopes are negative and so solutions are decreasing
Equilibirum solutions appear to be at $y = 0$ which is unstable and $y = 1$ which is semi-stable. 

\subsection*{Problems 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16}

\textbf{Problem 11} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.5} has an equilibrium solution at $y = 2$ to which all solutions converge to. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = 2 - y$.

\noindent\textbf{Problem 12} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.6} has an equilibrium solution at $y = 2$ which is unstable. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = y + 2$.

\noindent\textbf{Problem 13} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.7} has an equilibrium solution at $y = - 3$ which is stable. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = -3 - y$.

\noindent\textbf{Problem 14} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.8} has an equilibrium solution at $y = -1$ which is unstable. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = y + 1$.

\noindent\textbf{Problem 15} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.9} has an equilibrium solution at $y = 2$ which is stable and $y=0$ which is unstable. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = y(2 - y)$.

\noindent\textbf{Problem 16} The direction field of \textbf{Figure 1.1.10} has an equilibrium solution at $y = 0$ which is stable and $y = 3$ which is unstable. 
So the differential equation that corresponds to this direction field is $\frac{dy}{dx} = y(y - 3)$.
